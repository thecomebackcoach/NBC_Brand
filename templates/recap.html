01
Part one

The premise.

A short re-framing of what mission actually is, before you go back to your day.

The definition
// the whole thing, in one sentence

Forget vision, purpose, slogan, brand promise — set them aside for now. Mission, the way I use the word, is something simpler and harder.

How you want to leave everyone you come into contact with.

// that's it. that's the whole thing.

Notice what it isn't about. It isn't about you, or your business, or your product, or your service. It starts from what you want for the people you touch — clients, employees, friends, family, anyone who walks away from time with you. That orientation is the whole shift.

When that's clear, almost everything else gets easier. The right calls, the right hires, the right work, the right "no's" — they all follow from a settled answer to that one question.

02
Part two

Why it matters.

What Jim Collins found, and why mission is the line that separates the companies that lasted from the ones that didn't.

Built to last
// the pattern

Most companies don't make it past one decade.

Jim Collins studied the rare ones that did — the companies still standing at 80, 90, 100+ years. He wanted the common thread.

Most of the survivors stayed flat. A small subset did something else entirely — they kept climbing, decade after decade. And the thing that subset shared wasn't a product, or a market, or even a leader. It was a commitment statement. A mission, lived from the inside.

Three benefits of a clear one.

  1. 01 /
    The decision-making tool.

    You make decisions all day. Most of them aren't covered by policy. The mission gives you the next step when the manual runs out — the same way Disney's street sweeper, on seeing a child crying, puts down the broom and goes to deliver magic, fun, and happiness.

  2. 02 /
    The team alignment tool.

    The companies that last have an almost cult-like fanaticism to their mission. Not in a creepy way — in a clarifying way. People who fit lean in. People who don't either find their feet or take themselves out. You don't have to police it. The mission does.

  3. 03 /
    The thing that makes you loved.

    When every act, email, meeting, and product is held up against the mission, people can't help but feel it. They walk away lit up, telling someone else. The work compounds. Referrals stop being marketing and start being a side effect.

The prompts.

The questions we sat with together. Sit with them again — the answers move.

Start here
// three ways in

How do you want to leave people?

Three angles on the same question. Use whichever one cracks it open today; come back to the others next week.

  1. 01 /
    Everyone you come into contact with — clients, employees, even people who've left. How do you want them to feel when they walk away?
  2. 02 /
    Your closest, dearest person — after a lunch together. How would you want to leave them, such that you could "shoot me now" and you'd be okay with that?
  3. 03 /
    Your children — if they were left this way in life, would you be content?

05
Part five

How this works.

Seven things to keep in mind as you work with what you've got.

Operating principles
// the seven · part one of two
  1. 01 /
    It's internal.

    Your mission is for you and your team. No one outside needs to understand it or even know it. You can choose to share it externally — but you don't have to. "Remi moments." "Heli." Internal language is part of the magic.

  2. 02 /
    No format rules.

    One word. Three words. A sentence. A phrase. The form doesn't matter — the shift in your body when you say it does. You'll know you have it when you can't help but smile when you say it out loud.

  3. 03 /
    It's a heart process, not a head one.

    The first words will come from your head — sensible, polished, clever. Keep going. Go deeper into the unknown until the thing that comes up makes you feel something. The heart won't let you fake it.

  4. 04 /
    Watch your body, not your mouth.

    Your nervous system is the truth-teller. When you nail it, you'll feel a wave — energy, excitement, the urge to grin. If you can say it without smiling, you haven't got it yet.

// nick banks consulting for {{CLIENT_NAME_LOWER}} · principles 1–4 —
Operating principles
// the seven · part two of two
  1. 05 /
    Don't wait for perfect.

    There may never be a perfect articulation. Each working session, choose a "current mission" you'll work with for now. It will evolve. Disney, Sony, the long-lasting ones — they all rewrote theirs over time. Move with it.

  2. 06 /
    Start at the centre, then move out.

    Live it with yourself first. Then your immediate team. Then your clients, suppliers, partners. The further out you try to push it before it's lived at the centre, the faster it dies as words on a page.

  3. 07 /
    Name a mission manager.

    Inside a company, give someone the job of keeping the mission front and centre. Their role is to notice when the company drifts out of integrity with it — and to bring it back. Without that role, the mission becomes a poster on the wall.

Now what
// the homework, gently

Try it on.

Take what you've already got — the words on page five, the threads on page six — and live with them for a while.

  1. 01 /
    Sit with the words.

    For the next week, read your list once a day. No editing. Just notice which ones still flicker and which ones have already gone flat.

  2. 02 /
    Try one on for a week.

    Pick a current working version — it doesn't have to be the final one — and run a few decisions through it. A hire. A pricing call. A "yes" or "no" you've been avoiding. See what happens.

  3. 03 /
    Tell one person.

    Say it out loud to someone whose read you trust. Not for their edits — for your own body. Watch what your face does when you say it. That's the data.

  4. 04 /
    Come back to it.

    This is the soil, not the final tree. We'll keep working it together. When something shifts — or stays stubbornly stuck — that's the time to talk.
